# Task 09 – Nuclear fission and binding energy

## Problem Statement

1. Explain the concept of mass defect.
2. Calculate the energy released for a mass defect of $\Delta m=0.2 \, \mathrm{u}$.
3. Compare the energy with a chemical reaction.
4. Explain why the maximum of the binding energy occurs around iron.

## Theory

The mass of a nucleus is always less than the sum of the masses of its constituent nucleons. This difference is the mass defect $\Delta m$.

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Mass Defect and Energy
The "missing" mass is converted into binding energy $B$ according to:

$$
E = \Delta m c^2
$$

### 2. Calculation
Given $\Delta m = 0.2 \, \mathrm{u}$ and $1 \, \mathrm{u} \approx 931.5 \, \mathrm{MeV/c^2}$:

$$
E = 0.2 \times 931.5 \, \mathrm{MeV} = 186.3 \, \mathrm{MeV}
$$

### 3. Comparison
A typical chemical reaction (like combustion) releases $\sim 1 \text{--} 10 \, \mathrm{eV}$ per atom. Nuclear fission releases $\sim 200 \, \mathrm{MeV}$ per nucleus. Nuclear reactions are approximately $10^7$ times more energetic.

## Final Result

* **Energy Released:** $\approx 186 \, \mathrm{MeV}$.
* **Comparison:** Million-fold increase over chemical energy.

## Interpretation

The Binding Energy per Nucleon curve peaks at **Iron-56** ($^{56}\mathrm{Fe}$). 
* **Light nuclei:** Fusion increases binding energy (strong force range).
* **Heavy nuclei:** Fission increases binding energy (Coulomb repulsion dominates).
Iron is the most stable nucleus because it represents the optimal balance between the short-range strong nuclear attraction and the long-range electrostatic repulsion.
