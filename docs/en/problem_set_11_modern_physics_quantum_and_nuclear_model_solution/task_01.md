# Task 01 – Wave-particle duality (de Broglie)

## Problem Statement

An electron moves with a velocity of $v = 2.0 \times 10^6 \, \mathrm{m/s}$.

1. Calculate the momentum of the electron.
2. Calculate the de Broglie wavelength.
3. Compare it with the typical size of an atom ($\sim 10^{-10} \, \mathrm{m}$).
4. How will $\lambda$ change when the velocity is doubled?
5. Will the wave effect be easier or harder to observe?

## Theory

The de Broglie hypothesis states that every moving particle exhibits wave-like properties. The relationship between the momentum $p$ of a particle and its associated wavelength $\lambda$ is given by:

$$
\lambda = \frac{h}{p}
$$

For a non-relativistic particle of mass $m$ and velocity $v$, the momentum is:

$$
p = mv
$$

Constants used:
* Planck constant: $h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \mathrm{J \cdot s}$
* Electron mass: $m_e \approx 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \mathrm{kg}$

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Momentum Calculation
Using the classical formula for momentum:

$$
p = (9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \mathrm{kg}) (2.0 \times 10^6 \, \mathrm{m/s})
$$

$$
p \approx 1.82 \times 10^{-24} \, \mathrm{kg \cdot m/s}
$$

### 2. de Broglie Wavelength
Substituting the momentum into the de Broglie relation:

$$
\lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \mathrm{J \cdot s}}{1.82 \times 10^{-24} \, \mathrm{kg \cdot m/s}}
$$

$$
\lambda \approx 3.64 \times 10^{-10} \, \mathrm{m}
$$

### 3. Comparison with Atomic Scale
The typical size of an atom (Bohr radius or lattice spacing) is approximately $10^{-10} \, \mathrm{m}$ (1 Å). The calculated wavelength $\lambda \approx 3.64 \times 10^{-10} \, \mathrm{m}$ is of the same order of magnitude as the atomic dimensions.

### 4. Effect of Doubling Velocity
The wavelength is inversely proportional to velocity:

$$
\lambda \propto \frac{1}{v}
$$

If $v \to 2v$, then $\lambda \to \frac{1}{2}\lambda$. The wavelength is halved.

## Final Result

* **Momentum:** $p \approx 1.82 \times 10^{-24} \, \mathrm{kg \cdot m/s}$
* **Wavelength:** $\lambda \approx 3.64 \times 10^{-10} \, \mathrm{m}$
* **Scaling:** Doubling velocity reduces the wavelength by half.

## Interpretation

The fact that the electron's wavelength is comparable to the size of an atom means that electrons at this velocity will undergo significant diffraction when interacting with crystalline solids. As the velocity increases, the wavelength decreases. Because wave effects like diffraction are most prominent when the wavelength is comparable to the scale of the obstacles or openings, a smaller wavelength (due to higher velocity) makes the wave nature **harder** to observe in an atomic context.
