# Task 09 – Ideal vs. real transformer

## Problem Statement

Explain how a transformer works, define its ideal properties versus reality, identify the source of secondary voltage, list the types of losses, and provide typical values for the turns ratio and efficiency.

## Theory

A transformer is a passive electrical device that transfers electrical energy between two or more circuits through electromagnetic induction. It consists of a primary coil, a secondary coil, and typically a ferromagnetic core linking the two. 

According to Faraday's law of induction, a time-varying magnetic flux through a coil induces an electromotive force. 

$$
\mathcal{E} = -N \frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

Explain the operating principle. An alternating current flowing through the primary coil creates a continuously changing magnetic field. The ferromagnetic core directs this changing magnetic flux to pass through the secondary coil. The varying flux induces an alternating voltage in the secondary coil.

Identify the source of the secondary voltage. It is generated strictly by the time-varying magnetic flux $d\Phi/dt$ linking the secondary turns, a direct application of Faraday's law.

Define an ideal transformer. An ideal transformer assumes 100% efficient energy transfer. It operates under the following theoretical conditions:
* Zero electrical resistance in the coil windings.
* Perfect magnetic coupling (no flux leakage; all flux generated by the primary passes through the secondary).
* Infinite magnetic permeability of the core.
* Zero energy dissipation in the core itself.

Under these ideal conditions, the voltage ratio is directly proportional to the turns ratio:

$$
\frac{U_2}{U_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1} = n
$$

Describe a real transformer and its losses. In reality, transformers lose a small fraction of energy through several mechanisms:
* **Copper losses:** Joule heating due to the non-zero electrical resistance of the wire windings ($I^2R$ losses).
* **Flux leakage:** Some magnetic field lines fail to link both coils, escaping into the surrounding air.
* **Eddy current losses:** The changing magnetic field induces circulating currents within the conductive iron core, generating unwanted heat. 
* **Hysteresis losses:** Energy is expended repeatedly aligning and realigning the magnetic domains in the core material with each AC cycle.

Detail typical values. 
* **Turns ratio ($n$):** Varies drastically by application. Step-up transformers at power plants can have $n > 100$, while step-down transformers for consumer electronics often have $n < 0.05$.
* **Efficiency:** Real transformers are highly efficient. Large power grid transformers often exceed 98% to 99% efficiency.

## Final Result

1. A transformer works via mutual electromagnetic induction facilitated by a shared magnetic core.
2. The secondary voltage originates from the rate of change of magnetic flux.
3. Ideal properties assume zero resistance and perfect flux linkage; real transformers experience copper, leakage, eddy current, and hysteresis losses.
4. Turns ratios vary widely based on step-up or step-down needs, but efficiency is typically very high (>95%).

## Interpretation

The transformer is fundamental to modern alternating current grids. By easily stepping voltage up (for low-current, low-loss long-distance transmission) and stepping it back down (for safe consumer use), it solves the primary engineering challenge of electrical distribution.
